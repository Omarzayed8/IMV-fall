Q1:How does Drucker define the term "interface"? How about the process of designing interfaces? What is her opinion of interface design processes as they are generally handled today?

Drucker defines the term "interface" as a communication space between a user and a system, where the user interacts with the system through a set of controls or commands. Drucker argues that designing interfaces should be grounded in humanistic principles, which involve building on the critical study of the subject in literary, media, and visual studies. She believes that interface design should focus on the ways in which interfaces produce subjects of enunciation, rather than treating users as consumers. Drucker is critical of interface design processes as they are generally handled today, arguing that they are too focused on engineering and the system's needs, rather than the needs of the user. She believes that interface design should be more focused on the user experience and should incorporate humanistic principles. Drucker mentions the Van Gogh Correspondence project as an example of an interface that incorporates humanistic principles in its organization. The project offers a view into the repository that is structured by categories that come from critical study, editing, bibliography, or other traditions rooted in the appreciation and engagement with cultural materials.

Q2:What is Drucker's notion of "Humanistic Design"?

Drucker's notion of "Humanistic Design" involves designing interfaces that are grounded in humanistic principles, such as the critical study of the subject in literary, media, and visual studies. This approach focuses on the ways in which interfaces produce subjects of enunciation, rather than treating users as consumers. Drucker discussed the concept of the term subject-oriented interface which is one of the principles of humanistic design, meaning that it is concerned with the ways in which interfaces shape and produce subjects. This approach is in contrast to user-centered design, which tends to focus on accommodating user preferences and habits of thought. A subject-oriented approach to interface design takes into account the cultural systems and contexts in which interfaces are situated, and seeks to create interfaces that are responsive to the needs and desires of human subjects.
